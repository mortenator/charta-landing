Waterfall charts are the default language of business storytelling. Revenue bridges. Budget variances. Headcount changes quarter over quarter. If you've spent time in consulting, finance, or any ops role, you know the format. The problem has always been making them.

Google Slides has no native waterfall chart type. Google Sheets does — but the output looks like Google Sheets: default colors, no connector lines, no bar labels. Fine for internal use, not for a client deck. And every time a number changes, you go back to Sheets, update the chart, re-paste it.

Here's the fast way.

---

## What a waterfall chart actually shows

A waterfall chart visualizes sequential changes to a starting value. Start with revenue, subtract costs, subtract taxes, end with net income. Or: start with January headcount, add hires, subtract attrition, land on December headcount.

The power is in the intermediate steps. A bar chart shows start and end. A waterfall shows every bridge between them — which is the whole story.

Consultants reach for waterfall charts because they force clear thinking. You can't build one without knowing exactly what moved and why. Every bar is an answer to a question someone in the room is about to ask.

## Formatting tips for consulting-quality output

**Color:** Positive bars in green or your brand accent. Negative bars in red. Start and end bars in neutral gray — they represent totals, not changes.

**Bar labels:** Show the value on each bar, not just on the axis. Decision-makers read charts fast. "$320K expansion" on the bar itself beats hunting for it on the axis.

**Connector lines:** Thin gray lines connecting the top of each bar to the base of the next. Without them, floating bars look broken. Charta adds these automatically.

**Title:** Write what the chart proves, not what it shows. "Q4 revenue grew despite 12% churn" beats "Q4 Revenue Waterfall." No AI should be writing your slide headlines.

## Waterfall vs. bridge chart: what's the difference

These terms get used interchangeably. The distinction is worth knowing.

A **waterfall chart** builds up to a final total from a starting point. Revenue, cash, budget — any absolute value flowing through additions and subtractions.

A **bridge chart** (variance bridge) shows the difference between two known states. Last year's EBITDA vs. this year's, with each driver in between. Same visual format, different framing.

In practice: if you're explaining what caused a change between two totals, it's a bridge. If you're building toward a final total from a starting point, it's a waterfall. Charta handles both with the same prompt syntax.
